Question 1: What area do you want to depict in your map? Why?

I chose the area of the Motagua Fault zone in Guatemala because it is the only known source of the mineral jadeite, commonly known as jade, in the region (Figure 3, below). Jade was an important trade item in prehistoric Mesoamerica since the time of the Olmecs, and until recently there has been a raging debate on how many sources of jade are represented in the Mesoamerican samples. Two "types" have been recognized: the Olmec blue and Guatemalan green. The color differences (seen below in Figures 4 and 5) are so striking that researchers have proposed sources for jadeite (the mineral that predominates in jade rock) in Costa Rica and/or Mexico. However, jadeite is only formed in very specific geologic settings, namely low temperature and pressure subduction zones, locations that are very restricted globally (Figure 2, below). During the 1980s, several researchers found chemical differences between jade samples from Mesoamerica and argued that an unknown sample must exist. However, geologists, arguing from a processual stance, said that the only place in the region that could possibly make jadeite is in the Motagua Fault zone in Guatemala. Just as liquid water and/or ice can only occur in certain temperature and pressure conditions, minerals are controlled in the same way. In 1985 Hurricane Mitch eroded several stream systems, exposing the "Olmec/Costa Rican Blue" source. The reason for the chemical differences is that the sources were produced by two distinct subduction activities, one occurring between 77-65 Mya and the other from 125-113 Mya.
