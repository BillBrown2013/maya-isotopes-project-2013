Fall 2013 Problems Class Project Description My goal for this project is to create an online GIS modeling the spatial distribution of rates of caries (dental cavities), porotic hyperostosis, ratios of the stable isotopes 13C/12C and 15N/14N, and unstable isotope ratios of Sr/Rb in the Classic Maya world in Central America. Traditionally, bioarchaeologists, when making normative assessments of the effects of maize agriculture on prehistoric communities, have associated increasing rates of tooth decay (caries) and anemias (porotic hyperostosis) with increasing amounts of maize in the diet, because the carbohydrates in corn interact with chemicals in the mouth to form sugars and acids that weaken tooth enamel, leading to cavities. Increasing rates of anemias, most commonly seen in the crania of farming populations in the form of cribra orbitalia and porotic hyperostosis, have also traditionally been interpreted as evidence of over-reliance on maize because the anemias are thought to be the result of insufficient iron in the diet; an unbalanced diet in which maize is the predominant food has a double whammy effect in the sense that it reflects a lack of meat consumed, which lowers iron in the body, but there is also something about corn that actually blocks iron absorption in human physiology. The upshot is that decades of bioarchaeological research (eg Cohen and Armelegos 1984, etc) see increasing rates of caries and iron deficient anemias as the result of too much maize in the diets of prehistoric populations. In the early 1980s, archaeologists began using stable isotope ratios from apatite and collagen in human bones to pursue several lines of research, mainly reconstruction of paleo-diet. Photosynthesis in plants follows two basic pathways, the C3 and the C4 paths; maize follows the C4 pathway which tends to enrich the bones in the people who consume the plant in 13C; so theoretically people who eat a lot of corn or other domesticates should have bone apatite and collagen that are enriched in 13C relative to 12C. The ratio of 13C/12C has become the new proxy for maize reliance, and, if caries is another reliable proxy for maize consumption, then there should be a statistical correlation between rates of cavities and tooth decay with higher rates of 13C relative to 12C. Also, higher rates of 15N relative to 14N are interpreted as representing more meat in prehistoric diets; if anemias seen in the crania of ancient people reflect a lack of protein/meat in the diet, then there should be lower rates of 15N to 14N in the skeletal remains of farming communities than in those of hunter-gatherers. Another possibly unrelated topic is that ratios of the unstable isotope Sr/Rb reflect that of where a person was born and weaned, which is important because Sr/Rb ratios are thought to reflect where people were born across a landscape and thus could potentially be a way to map human migration throughout Mesoamerica (the geology of Central America has different ratios of Sr/Rb as one moves inland, so it is possible to distinguish native born people from immigrants based on Sr/Rb ratios). THESIS: If increasing rates of caries represent increasing reliance on maize agriculture, and higher ratios of 13C to 12C represent the same thing, then the two should be statistically correlated. Further, if increasing rates of iron-deficient anemias reflect a lack of meat in the diet, and lower ratios of 15N to 14N represent a lack of meat/protein in the diet, then these two factors should be statistically correlated as well. WHAT I WANT TO DO: I want to create an interactive map that would show the distribution through time and space of rates of caries, anemias, 13C/12C ratios, 15/14N ratios, population densities, and possibly Sr/Rb ratios at known Maya sites. A brief perusal of bioarchaeological literature pertaining to Classic Maya shows that researchers do not agree on how healthy the Maya were during this time; Rebecca Storey (1993) states that rates of enamel hypoplasia and cribra orbitalia reflect “a society on the verge of decline”, whereas other skeletal series in the Maya world paint a radically different picture. When broaching the topic of Maya health based on skeletal remains, workers typically tabulate pertinent data (ie, rates of caries and anemias) and/or ratios of 13C/12C, but I feel that an GIS driven map would be a much more effective tool in showing these data. Typically workers will show tables of rates of caries of various Maya sites such as Tikal, Copan, etc., but I feel these data would be better represented on an interactive GIS map where the various factors could be geospatially analyzed; the ability to invoke other variables through web mapping services may lead to a greater understanding of the “state of the nation” of the Classic Maya. For instance, periodontal disease which also contributes to rates of caries, is communicable, so perhaps settlement nucleation plays a role in rates of tooth decay. Ultimately I would like to produce a GIS with a few base map options for the viewer, but several layers that could be added/subtracted so that bioarchaeologists could have the freedom to explore other explanations for the osteological data rather than the traditional ones (ie, too much maize in the diet). In the perfect case scenario I would also like to derive a GIS that shows the distribution of Sr/Rb ratios throughout Central America, so that when other workers conduct that isotopic analysis, they can ball park where the person came from, but that may be out of the scope of this class. Data Requirements I would need data on 13C/12C and 15/14N ratios and rates of caries and anemias, which I don’t know if I can find. Then, I want to acquire several datasets to act as base layers, including BMNG, Google Maps, and Open Layers. maps. Also I want to add several overlay layers, providing the potential to explore other avenues of explanation, meaning, maybe there are other geographical and/or social factors that could explain the isotopic and skeletal data (such as settlement density). Obviously the addition of topographic, soil, and hydrological data would be critical to this project, and I would like to learn how to access these data through remote web mapping services. Proposed Products Ultimately I would like to produce an interactive website called the Maya Health Project that would incorporate all of these factors into a GIS that would allow viewers to manipulate the data in unpredictable ways. The freedom to overlay different layers and conduct geospatial analyses could potentially lead to explanations that are unthought of now. The upshot is that after pouring through the bioarchaeological literature of the Classic Maya, I feel that we need to model this data in a GIS format that would allow “thinking outside the box”.
